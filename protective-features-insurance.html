US State Laws Regulating Investigative Genetic Genealogy (FIGG)

State Comparisons of Protective Features

Overview

Since 2018, lawmakers, law enforcement, defense counsel, and others have debated whether and how law enforcement should be able to tap consumer genetics platforms for investigative purposes. Forensic Investigative Genetic Genealogy (“FIGG”) involves comparing crime scene DNA data to DNA profiles held by consumer genetic platforms to find individuals distantly related to a possible perpetrator. By compiling extensive family trees, investigators hope to identify the individual within the family tree most likely to be the source of the crime scene evidence. FIGG burst into public view with the arrest of the Golden State Killer, and law enforcement throughout the country have since used this method to identify suspects in nearly a thousand cases. As use of FIGG has expanded, so too have the types of cases in which this method has been pursued.

While many states do not have relevant laws on the books, some states have begun to engage in efforts to regulate and formalize law enforcement use of FIGG. Most notably, Maryland and Utah have enacted comprehensive regulatory frameworks governing this investigative method. As other legislators consider regulation in this space, they should be mindful of several features that can make legislation more or less protective of genetic privacy, while still authorizing appropriate law enforcement use. Seven protective features are set out below.

1. Regulate law enforcement directly

Several states have enacted legislation intended to protect privacy for users of consumer genetics platforms. Most of these laws direct consumer genetics platforms to provide clear notice of their privacy practices that are written in plain language, obtain express consent from consumers for using and disclosing genetic data, and establish legal policies for non-consensual data disclosure to law enforcement. But a handful of states regulate law enforcement directly, for instance by requiring law enforcement to seek familial matches only through platforms that obtain user consent to law enforcement use.

Compare . . .

California Law Utah Law
(a) To safeguard the privacy, confidentiality, security, and integrity of a consumer’s genetic data, a direct-to-consumer genetic testing company shall do both of the following:
  (1) Provide clear and complete information regarding the company’s policies and procedures for the collection, use, maintenance, and disclosure, as applicable, of genetic data
  (2) Obtain a consumer’s express consent for collection, use, and disclosure of the consumer’s genetic data . . . .

Cal. Civ. Code § 56.181 (emphasis added) 		(5) When requesting an investigative genetic genealogy service or genetic genealogy database utilization from a genetic genealogy company . . . , a law enforcement agency shall:
  (a) disclose to the genetic genealogy company that the request is from a law enforcement agency;
  (b) only make a request to a genetic genealogy company that:
    (i) provides notice to the genetic genealogy company's service users and the public that law enforcement may use the genetic genealogy company's services to investigate crimes or to identify unidentified human remains . . . .

Utah Code § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

These provisions appear to have the same goal—restricting law enforcement to platforms and users who have given consent—but they may have very different results. After all, in several cases already litigated, investigators have sought familial matches in databases that do not permit law enforcement use or without disclosing their law enforcement status so as to seek matches even among users who have declined law enforcement use. Regulating platforms is unlikely to deter zealous law enforcement investigators. Regulating law enforcement directly, however, minimizes the risk of such deceptive behavior by making it unlawful.

2. Include a Role for Judicial Oversight

Whether law enforcement must obtain judicial authorization before engaging in FIGG can reinforce accountability for compliance with statutory privacy protections. Judicial oversight introduces a neutral third-party decisionmaker outside of law enforcement, enhancing trust in the system and promoting good policymaking by balancing investigative needs with individual rights. States have incorporated judicial oversight in different ways.

States that regulate law enforcement conduct directly have taken divergent approaches to judicial oversight at the initiation of a FIGG search. Montana requires either user consent or a warrant for some searches, while implying that a warrant is always required for FIGG. Maryland requires judicial authorization of law enforcement’s use of FIGG in every case and specifies the kinds of cases for which authorization is permitted. This authorization is distinct from the general standards for subpoenas or warrants. Finally, Utah does not require any judicial oversight before law enforcement undertakes FIGG.

Compare . . .

Montana Law Maryland Law
(1) A government entity may not obtain DNA search results from a consumer DNA database:
   (a) without a search warrant issued by a court on a finding of probable cause; or
   (b) unless the consumer whose information is sought previously waived the consumer's right to privacy in the information.
(2) A government entity may not obtain familial DNA search results or search results from partial matching from the DNA identification index or a consumer DNA database without a search warrant issued by a court on a finding of probable cause.

Mont. Code Ann. § 44-6-104 (emphasis added)
 (a)(1) A forensic genetic genealogy investigation may not be initiated without judicial authorization and without certifying before the court that the forensic sample and the criminal case satisfy the criteria set forth in this section.

Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-102 (emphasis added)

Among states with statutes that regulate consumer genetics platforms (rather than law enforcement directly), the language surrounding legal process and court orders can be confusing. Some states prohibit platforms from disclosing user genetic data without the user’s consent unless served with a court order or warrant. Others merely require some form of “valid legal process” for such disclosures, which leaves room for misunderstanding and could include subpoenas issued without a court’s review or involvement. Even more confusingly, some states require “a valid legal process,” which might include processes internal to the consumer genetics platform itself.

Compare . . .

Minnesota Law Kentucky Law Arizona Law
(a) To safeguard the privacy, confidentiality, security, and integrity of a consumer's genetic data, a direct-to-consumer genetic testing company must: . . .
    (3) not disclose genetic data to law enforcement or any other governmental agency without a consumer's express written consent, unless the disclosure is made pursuant to a valid search warrant or court order . . .

Minn. Stat. Ann. § 325F.995(2)(a) (emphasis added) 		(2) To safeguard the privacy, confidentiality, security, and integrity of a consumer's genetic data, a direct-to-consumer genetic testing company shall: . . .
    (c) Require a valid legal process for disclosing genetic data to law enforcement or any other government agency without a consumer’s express written consent . . .

Ky. Rev. Stat. § 311.705 (emphasis added) 		A. A direct-to-consumer genetic testing company shall: . . .
3. Require a valid legal process for disclosing genetic data to law enforcement or any other government agency without a consumer's express written consent . . .


Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 44-8002 (emphasis added)

Finally, though Utah does not provide for judicial oversight of law enforcement’s use of FIGG, it does require judicial involvement if a convicted person seeks to use FIGG to prove their innocence. Maryland requires judicial oversight in both instances, as well as for cases in which a criminal defendant (for yet convicted) seeks to use FIGG in their defense. In other states, defense or postconviction access to FIGG is unaddressed, leaving the power of this method unfairly only for the prosecution in most instances.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Utah Law
(a) A defendant in a criminal case charged with a crime of violence under § 14-101 of the Criminal Law Article or a defendant convicted of a crime of violence under § 14-101 of the Criminal Law Article and seeking postconviction DNA testing is entitled to seek judicial authorization for an FIGG by filing an affidavit with a trial court or postconviction court

Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-103 (emphasis added) 		(2) A law enforcement agency may request an investigative genetic genealogy service or a genetic genealogy database utilization from the bureau or a genetic genealogy company if: . . .
    (b) ordered by a court in accordance with a postconviction relief proceeding under Section 78B-9-301.

Utah Code Ann. § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

3. Affirm individual control over investigative use of genetic data

Nearly all states that have enacted legislation relevant to consumer genetic privacy recognize the important role of an individual’s control over use of their genetic data. Some states limit law enforcement use to those platforms that obtain affirmative consent from users for law enforcement matching. This kind of explicit consent is crucial for good policymaking because it ensures that users make a knowing and voluntary choice to participate in law enforcement efforts.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Utah Law
(d) FIGG may only be conducted using a direct-to-consumer or publicly available open-data personal genomics database that:
    (1) provides explicit notice to its service users and the public that law enforcement may use its service sites to investigate crimes or to identify human remains; and
    (2) seeks acknowledgement and express consent from its service users regarding the substance of the notice described in item (1) of this subsection.

Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-102 (emphasis added) 		(5) When requesting an investigative genetic genealogy service or genetic genealogy database utilization from a genetic genealogy company . . . , a law enforcement agency shall:
    (a) disclose to the genetic genealogy company that the request is from a law enforcement agency;
    (b) only make a request to a genetic genealogy company that:
      (i) provides notice to the genetic genealogy company's service users and the public that law enforcement may use the genetic genealogy company's services to investigate crimes or to identify unidentified human remains;
      (ii) allows a user to:
        (A) opt in or out of having the user's data be accessible in an investigation requested by law enforcement; and
        (B) access the genetic genealogy company's services even if the user opts out of having the user's data be accessible in an investigation requested by law enforcement; and . . . .

Utah Code § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

Both Maryland and Utah’s policies expressly limit law enforcement use only to those platforms that seek and obtain consent from users for that type of use. Maryland’s requirement that platforms obtain “express consent” from users may go further than Utah’s requirement that users be able to “opt in or out” of law enforcement use. Utah’s formulation may allow law enforcement use of consumer genetics platforms that opt users into such use by default, while Maryland’s policy requires affirmative, opt in consent. Importantly, however, Utah’s policy specifies that users’ power to opt out of law enforcement searches be genuine. That is, Utah mandates that users must be able to opt out of law enforcement use without having to give up access to the platform as a whole. This is significant, as tying consent to law enforcement use to consent to use the platform at all undermines users’ free and voluntary choices regarding law enforcement use.

But most states, while purporting to “safeguard the privacy, confidentiality, security, and integrity of a consumer's genetic data,” permit law enforcement to get around a user’s lack of consent by using some alternate process. These provisions often use confusing, and confusingly similar, terms for a range of possible legal pathways.

4. Alternate Legal Pathways for Non-Consensual Disclosure

Despite claims to safeguard genetic privacy, many states permit law enforcement to bypass a user’s lack of consent by using alternative legal processes. These provisions often use confusing, similar language for a range of legal pathways.

Compare . . .

Minnesota Law Kentucky Law Arizona Law
To safeguard the privacy, confidentiality, security, and integrity of a consumer's genetic data, a direct-to-consumer genetic testing company must not disclose genetic data to law enforcement or any governmental agency without a consumer's express written consent, unless such disclosure is made pursuant to a valid search warrant or court order.
Minn. Stat. Ann. § 325F.995(2)(a) (emphasis added) 		The company shall require valid legal process for disclosing genetic data to law enforcement or any governmental agency without a consumer’s express written consent.
Ky. Rev. Stat. § 311.705 (emphasis added) 		The company shall require a valid legal process for disclosing genetic data to law enforcement or any governmental agency without a consumer's express written consent.
Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 44-8002 (emphasis added)

While states like Minnesota mandate a court order, others merely require some form of “valid legal process,” which can be open to interpretation. In Arizona, the phrasing suggests that even an internal process might suffice.

Additionally, some states abandon individual control altogether for law enforcement use – exempting such use from privacy protections, which may mislead the public about their rights.

Compare . . .

Kentucky Law Alabama Law
Requires valid legal process for disclosing genetic data to law enforcement or any governmental agency without a consumer’s express written consent.
Ky. Rev. Stat. § 311.705 (emphasis added) 		Section 8-43-3: Policies and Procedures Governing the Collection, Use, Maintenance, and Disclosure of Genetic Data; Consumer Consent Requirements.
Section 8-43-5: Applicability of Chapter – this chapter does not apply to: (3) Biological samples or genetic data lawfully obtained by law enforcement pursuant to a criminal investigation.
Ala. Code §§ 8-43-3 & 8-43-5 (emphasis added)

Alabama’s wholesale exclusion of law enforcement investigative use means its Genetic Data Privacy Act does not offer meaningful privacy protection in this area.

5. Protect Third Parties Who Are Not Suspects in the Crime

In FIGG investigations, gaps in a genealogical family tree may prompt law enforcement to collect DNA from individuals who are not themselves suspects but who may be related to a potential perpetrator. These rules govern how such third-party samples are obtained.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Utah Law
(f)(1)(i) Informed consent in writing shall be obtained from any third party whose DNA sample is sought for assisting a FIGG search, with the entire consent process documented via video or audio recording.
(ii) If obtaining consent would compromise the investigation—and the third party has not already refused—investigators may seek authorization to covertly collect the sample.
Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-102 (emphasis added) 		(3)(a) Before collecting a third-party DNA specimen for FIGG, a law enforcement agency shall:
  (i) Consult with the prosecuting agency; and
  (ii)(A) Obtain informed, voluntary consent from the individual; or
    (B) If consent is likely to compromise the investigation, obtain authorization for covert collection from the prosecuting agency.
Utah Code Ann. § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

6. Make FIGG Available to Prove Innocence, Not Just Guilt

Fair access to FIGG is essential for criminal defendants. Defense and postconviction counsel often encounter obstacles when trying to obtain crime scene evidence for testing because such evidence is typically controlled by law enforcement—which may be reluctant to cooperate—creating an imbalance that disadvantages defendants.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Utah Law
A defendant charged with or convicted of a crime of violence under § 14-101, seeking postconviction DNA testing, is entitled to seek judicial authorization for FIGG by filing an affidavit with the appropriate trial or postconviction court.
Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-103 (emphasis added) 		A law enforcement agency may request an investigative genetic genealogy service or database utilization if ordered by a court in a postconviction relief proceeding under Section 78B-9-301.
Utah Code Ann. § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

7. Impose Genuine Consequences for Violations

Enforcing real consequences for breaches of consumer genetic privacy is critical for accountability and deterring misconduct. Some states allow individuals to sue for violations, while others empower state officials to enforce the law. Minor or unenforceable penalties, however, may not sufficiently deter wrongdoing.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Kentucky Law Utah Law
(k) A person whose genetic genealogy information, FIGG profile, or DNA sample is wrongfully disclosed, collected, or maintained in violation of this title has a private right of action and is entitled to minimum liquidated damages of $5,000.
Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-102 (emphasis added) 		The attorney general may bring an action to enforce this chapter.
Ky. Rev. Stat. § 311.705 (emphasis added) 		(8)(b)(i) If a court finds that a law enforcement employee or agent knowingly violated a provision, that person shall be barred from participating in FIGG searches for one year.
(ii) This finding does not constitute a judgment for monetary damages or attorney fees.
Utah Code Ann. § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

Among these approaches, authorizing individual lawsuits provides the most robust protection, while temporary bans alone may be less effective at deterring misconduct.

8. Require Public Reporting and Review

Public reporting and review are vital for transparency and accountability. Such reporting enables legislators and the public to understand how consumer genetic information is used in FIGG investigations. Yet few statutes require this level of transparency.

Compare . . .

Maryland Law Utah Law
(a) On or before June 1 annually, the Governor's Office of Crime Prevention and Policy shall submit a publicly available report to the Governor and the General Assembly for the preceding calendar year, including:
  (1) the number of FIGG requests, broken down by prosecutors, pretrial defendants, and postconviction defendants;
  [(2)-(10) various case features]
(b) A panel comprising judges, prosecutors, defense attorneys, public defenders, law enforcement officials, crime laboratory directors, bioethicists, racial justice experts, criminal justice researchers, and civil/ privacy rights organizations shall review the report and make policy recommendations.
Md. Code Ann., Crim. Proc. § 17-105 (West) (emphasis added) 		(2)(a) Beginning on January 1, 2024, a law enforcement agency shall annually, on or before April 30, submit a report to the commission for the preceding calendar year, including:
  (i) the number of genetic genealogy database utilizations requested; and
  (ii) [various case features]
(4) The commission shall take specified actions, and
(c) On or before August 1, publish the report on its website.
Utah Code Ann. § 53-10-403.7 (emphasis added)

Both Maryland and Utah require detailed reporting on FIGG usage. Maryland goes further by mandating an interdisciplinary review panel to inform policy.
